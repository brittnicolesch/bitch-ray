# The Woman Who Saw Everything: Louella Rabuyo and the Question No One Will Answer

*How Epstein's housekeeper became the operations manager — and what she must have known*

---

**She was there when the girls arrived.**

**She made the beds they slept in.**

**She scheduled the dinners with governors and billionaires.**

**She coordinated the flights, managed the households, supervised the staff who cleaned the rooms where it all happened.**

**And for fifteen years, she claims she never saw a thing.**

---

Louella Rabuyo wasn't just Jeffrey Epstein's housekeeper. That word — "housekeeper" — does the work of minimizing what she actually was: the operations manager of a criminal enterprise, the logistical coordinator who kept the machine running, the trusted insider who had keys to every door and access to every schedule.

The Epstein Files, released in waves over the past year, contain thousands of pages of emails, wire transfers, deposition transcripts, and flight logs. They paint a picture not of a peripheral cleaning lady, but of a woman at the operational center of Epstein's life — someone who knew his routines better than almost anyone, who handled his calendar, who interfaced with his most powerful guests, and who was trusted enough to be named in his will and paid for years after his death.

The question isn't whether Louella Rabuyo knew what Jeffrey Epstein was doing in his homes. 

The question is how anyone could possibly believe she didn't.

## The Hiring: Ghislaine's Pick

November 2004. Louella Rabuyo walks into 358 El Brillo Way, Palm Beach — Jeffrey Epstein's mansion — for a job interview. The person conducting the interview is Ghislaine Maxwell.

Not a staffing agency. Not HR. Ghislaine Maxwell herself.

This is how it works in Epstein's world: Maxwell doesn't just recruit girls. She recruits the infrastructure. She picks the people who will keep the house running, who will see things and say nothing, who will maintain the veneer of normalcy while the operation unfolds around them.

Louella starts at $32,000 a year. She's told she'll get annual raises. Her job, officially: "tidying, beds, laundry, attending to guests."

But within months, her role expands. Emails from the Epstein Files show her coordinating logistics, managing junior staff, relaying messages, handling Epstein's schedule. By 2016, she's the one organizing dinners with New Mexico Governor Bill Richardson. By 2018, Richard Kahn — Epstein's top accountant — is asking her to take over senior household operations as other long-term staff depart.

This isn't a woman folding towels. This is a woman running the household.

## The Access She Had

Let's be specific about what Louella Rabuyo's job actually entailed, based on the documentary record:

**She coordinated Jeffrey Epstein's schedule.** On September 19, 2016, she forwarded a message from Epstein to household staff: "Gov Richardson dinner tonight at 7 pm." The same email thread includes a note to Karyna Shuliak, another key household coordinator. She wasn't just receiving information — she was distributing it, managing logistics, ensuring the operation ran smoothly.

**She supervised the cleaning staff.** The women who cleaned the rooms where abuse occurred reported to her. She managed their schedules, their assignments, their work. She decided who cleaned what and when.

**She lived in Epstein's Manhattan apartment building.** For work trips to New York, she was assigned Apartment 7J at 301 East 66th Street — a building originally owned by Les Wexner and later controlled by Jeffrey's brother Mark. That building was the Manhattan nerve center for the network. Unit 7J had previously been used by Jean-Luc Brunel, the modeling agent and accused trafficker. Other residents: Rich Kahn (Epstein's accountant), Larry Visoski (pilot), Sarah Kellen (Epstein's executive assistant and recruiter), Karyna Shuliak.

Louella wasn't visiting that building. She had a unit there. She was part of the infrastructure.

**She had a front-row seat to the operation.** In her 2009 deposition, Louella acknowledged she saw "this many young females come over to his house every day." She testified she saw photographs of topless young women in Epstein's closet. She said she learned about the abuse allegations "from the news" and insisted, "I don't know what's happening in the bedroom."

But here's what we know from victim testimony: Epstein's abuse didn't just happen in bedrooms. It happened in massage rooms. Living rooms. Pool areas. Bathrooms. Common spaces that housekeepers clean, monitor, and maintain.

**She was trusted at the highest level.** When Palm Beach police executed a search warrant on Epstein's mansion, who called Louella that morning? Ghislaine Maxwell. "Louella, you can report in the afternoon," Maxwell told her — delaying her usual 8-9 AM arrival until after law enforcement had come and gone.

This is coordination between co-conspirators. This is what it looks like when someone in the network alerts another insider to stay away while authorities are on site.

## The Money Trail

On August 3, 2015, two transactions occurred on the same day:

1. A $130,000 wire transfer from Ghislaine Maxwell's personal savings account to the TerraMar Project at UBS
2. A $130,000 cash purchase of a condominium — Bent Tree Villas West, Unit 1-101-A, Florida — from Florence McNiff to Louella Rabuyo

The timing is exact. The amount is exact. The seller, Florence McNiff, is now deceased — making it impossible to ask her directly who provided the purchase funds.

But in April 2015, months before the deed was recorded, Louella sent an email to Jeffrey Epstein thanking him for "the roof over my head." She referenced a realtor, an inspection, HOA paperwork, and a June 15 closing date.

Louella lives in that home today.

She was also named in Epstein's trust. She received payments after his death in 2019. Attorney Bradley Edwards, representing Epstein's victims, identified her as a civil co-conspirator and noted that her legal representation — like that of Sarah Kellen, Nadia Marcinkova, Ghislaine Maxwell, and Epstein's pilots — was paid for by Jeffrey Epstein.

Edwards called it "nearly impossible to take a deposition of someone that would have helpful information that was not represented by an attorney paid for by Epstein." This wasn't legal defense. It was institutional protection. It was a coordinated effort to ensure that the people who knew what happened inside those homes would never tell the full story.

## The Impossibility of Not Knowing

Here's what we're being asked to believe:

That a woman who worked in Epstein's Palm Beach home for fifteen years, who supervised the staff who cleaned every room, who scheduled his dinners and managed his logistics, who was copied on emails about flights and guest arrangements, who lived in his New York apartment building alongside his pilots and schedulers and assistants, who saw young women coming to the house "every day," who found photographs of topless young women in his closet, who was called by Ghislaine Maxwell on the morning of a police raid and told to come in late — that this woman somehow had no idea what Jeffrey Epstein was doing.

That she was present, proximate, trusted, and observant enough to run the household operations for one of the most powerful men in the world, but oblivious to the activity that victims describe as pervasive, frequent, and occurring in common areas of the homes she managed.

That she saw nothing, heard nothing, suspected nothing.

It's not credible.

And yet she has never been charged. She has never been subpoenaed by Congress. She has never been required to answer, under oath and under penalty of perjury, the questions that victims and investigators and journalists have been asking for years.

## What Needs to Happen

Louella Rabuyo is not a bystander. She is a person who held operational authority in a trafficking network, who benefited financially from her role, who received legal protection funded by the man running the operation, and who continues to live in a home that all available evidence suggests was purchased with funds that flowed through the same financial architecture that paid Robert Foos, Harry Beller, and the rest of the inside circle.

She deserves to be called before Congress.

She deserves to answer questions about what she saw, what she knew, and what she did with that knowledge.

She deserves to explain how the condominium was purchased, who provided the funds, and whether TerraMar — Ghislaine Maxwell's sham charity — was the mechanism that turned Epstein's money into real estate for the household staff who kept the operation running.

The victims of Jeffrey Epstein's crimes have been asked to recount, in excruciating detail, what was done to them. They've been cross-examined, doubted, smeared, and forced to relive trauma in courtrooms and depositions.

Louella Rabuyo has been asked almost nothing.

That needs to change.
