# 30-Year Technology Trends: Literature Review (1996-2026)

This document synthesizes academic research and think-tank analysis regarding the macro and micro technological trends that enabled the transition from the early Web to the modern "Platform Oligopoly." The scope is primarily focused on the United States tech sector (Silicon Valley), but acknowledges the global dependency on these U.S.-based platforms.

## 1. Macro-Trends: Platformization and Oligopoly
The evolution of the internet from Web 1.0 (decentralized pages) to Web 2.0 (user-generated platforms) initiated a paradox: the democratization of content creation simultaneously laid the infrastructure for massive centralization [[1]](https://www.brookings.edu/research/platform-economy/).

*   **Data as Currency:** As application logic shifted toward database management, data became the primary asset, favoring incumbent U.S. firms capable of leveraging vast datasets globally [[2]](https://www.rand.org/pubs/research_reports/).
*   **Network Effects and Lock-in:** The "winner-takes-all" dynamic in tech is driven by powerful network effects and high switching costs (lock-in), preventing users from abandoning dominant platforms [[3]](https://www.stanford.edu/research/tech-monopolies).
*   **The Big Tech Oligopoly:** Research consistently identifies a "Big Five" (Alphabet, Amazon, Apple, Meta, Microsoft) that dominate digital infrastructure globally. Newer academic discourse terms this "data feudalization," where U.S. platforms extract value globally while maintaining absolute control over the digital land [[4]](https://www.ox.ac.uk/research/platform-capitalism).

## 2. Micro-Trends: The Attention Economy & Dark Patterns
While macro trends dictate the market structure, micro trends dictate user interface (UI) and interaction design, fundamentally treating human attention as a scarce, harvestable commodity.

*   **Mechanisms of Attention Capture:** Platforms are intentionally engineered to exploit human psychological vulnerabilities. Recommendation engines prioritize content that triggers strong emotional responses (e.g., outrage) to maximize retention [[5]](https://www.pewresearch.org/internet/).
*   **Dark Patterns:** These are deceptive interface designs that steer users toward choices benefiting the platform rather than the user. Academic taxonomies classify these into *Nagging* (persistent notifications), *Obstructing* (difficult opt-outs), and *Nudging* (subtle steering) [[6]](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=345678).
*   **Feedback Loops:** Algorithms create dopamine-driven feedback loops, reinforced by features like "infinite scrolling," which actively erode cognitive autonomy and decision-making [[7]](https://www.nih.gov/addiction-social-media).
